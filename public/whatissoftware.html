What is software, the problem with immortality and how does it compare to my sewing machine ?


(a collection of thoughts I used to examine my assumptions about software, craftsmanship and meaning, while accidentally discovering some principles of design.)

(initial observations)
Lately I have been buying a lot of physical tools, mostly vintage and of unfathomably high quality in comparison to what is available on the market today. At the same time I'm starting to get frustrated with how software is being perceived and it is leading to some doubts and a rather contrasting image compared to the ever-lasting tools with which I can have an immediate, real world impact. Therefore it is adequate to take some time and think these things through before I jump to any conclusions.
Unlike physical goods modern software can change its form significantly from the state you purchased it in. This is a rather counter-intuitive phenomenon given the static nature of code itself. People are changing prices post-contractually, removing features, sometimes removing the purchased product alltogether, adding unskippable ads to your medium and so on. Unlike a saw, a vise, a house, a boat, a car, a pen, software usually cannot be used for a persons lifetime and I'm not convinced it has to be this way.
Sure, physical goods may break down before their useful life is over, however this has not been the standard for all of mankind. A Singer brand sewing machine from 1950 may work just as well now, as it did on the first day it was purchased 76 years ago, if the instructed care was provided to the machine. At this point, in 2026 many seem to assume that software is a transient good with limited expectations, amplified by the notion that a large language model might replace software engineering some day.

(physical vs virtual)
To conclude whether software is simply inferior to physical products, we need to first establish what a physical product is, what its actual purpose is and then do the same for software. Maybe we can even find examples of software that defy time and trends.
A physical product is a tool to interact with the physical world. Physical products serve humans to fulfill a task: to split wood, traverse space, shelter us from the rain. They allow humans to shape the world to their needs and increase their abilities to accomplish the tasks they want and need to get done.
A virtual product is a product to interact with the virtual world to achieve tasks unfit f or the physical world. That virtual world is a computer and networks of connected computers, a device to take human inputs and rapidly compute outputs. An unthinkable task for example is to perform very fast search in a large physical database (sure, divide and conquer but there is a limit). Try finding a book in a library as quickly as you can perform a search algorithm on a computer. (Your travel time to and from the library counts as well.) Let's not talk about managing patient file cards manually in a doctors office and everything that can go wrong in that scenario. It follows that the purpose of software is to implement digital solutions to physical problems. These solutions can be general or specific. They might be a calculator implementation, text editor or an e-mail program to interface with a network protocol and lay out data for humans to interact with. These specific programs re-implement basic features and in the same way an email program re-implements a text editor, a roofer's hammer and an engineer's hammer both implement a metalhead with a flat face on a stick. All differentiated by specific features such as weight and shape.

(problems with pricing perfection)
One attribute that is unique to software is the ability for it to never break, get dull or develop signs of use. This is also part of what makes software pricing difficult and why some companies appear to make software that is incomplete and broken, in need of repair. Software can be copied and perfectly replicated one to one on another system. You bought one drill bit but you can replicate it as often as you like and do so forever. It never breaks and never needs to be resharpened. That's a good product but a bad one to price to humans. Should that drill bit cost more th an a months rent ? Usually this means people will not want to afford it, even if it is reasonable over a lifetime and technically the correct purchase. Since you can copy it at no cost, one person buys it and supplies everyone with free copies after you crafted, pruned and shaped it meticulously to perfection, costing you many hours of your life. To a certain extent this model has been tried with early physical media by employing copyright protection and distributing activation keys to remedy this effect. The pricing issue is not an issue of the individual but a side effect of a societal model that is incompatible with perfection as is possible in software. We are organized to re-purchase consumables and commodities every month and people usually don't think about the lifetime of an asset or view their purchases as investments at all.

(physical things can be inherited but what about software?)
Software is immortal but the state of our world is not. There are parts of software like open standards, programming languages, typography that can be inherited and live on. Basic programs like Vi, Tar and software in the Linux ecosystem that is correctly written with foresight might still be functional today thanks to C's backward compatability. If a C compiler exists and all the dependencies are included in the program the old software may run, assuming the systems API's are still the same. The software may also not run if a programmer used undefined behavior and trickery to achieve certain functionality like using buffer overflows with 32Bit numbers. The simpler the correct software the more likely it is to still work in my estimation. Old errors may be detected by new compilers such that the compiler won't compile the code even though an older compiler would still have completed the job. Software and hardware that emulates the old system in its entirety may also be available. A hardware + software combination without third party authentication can live on for decades, see the GameBoy ecosystem or older consoles in general. Hand calculators, old CNC machines, firmware, purpose built software falls in that category. Additionally, one could say we inherited the Internet, a rather meaningful piece of technology.

(state of things)
Lately if you like to pay attention to voices on the Internet they would have you believe that software is a solved problem and turning into a commodity, yet I see software in many places degrading, while compute increases. Keyboards dont type correctly, passwords leak, year old bugs dont get fixed, data gets lost, CLI tools lag, search is broken... and so on. At the same time there are people hand crafting new debuggers for C++ and reading assembly code. In parallel I'm increasingly starting to notice that many physical goods have become poorly designed and of low quality, barely passable if at all. Outliers exist of course but I do suggest picking good design over bad design and being judgemental to counteract this trend. If we do respect our time enough to care about good design in the things we interact with everyday, our transient time on this planet becomes more meaningful. I suspect this is intuitive to most people but we are seeing contrary trends, at least in the broader sense.

(friend vs foe and good design)
Anyone who helps us in achieving our goals we call friend and anyone who actively defers our ability in doing so we may name our enemy. However we seem to not hold our physical and virtual goods to that same standard even though small inconveniences on a daily basis compound. While any tool is better than no tool, good design serves the human in one task mainly: achieving their goals as effortlessly as possible. This requires pruning and shaping the product until no further cutting out or adding to is necessary or reasonable. The point of sufficiency. That Singer 401G has reached sufficiency for the target audience in a world that didn't change in 75 years. Sure nylon and synthetics were added but sufficiency then meant sewing thick leather and heavy fabric and by accident this extends to todays requirements as well. If this is achieved, an emotional bond to the product will develop and we may find beauty in our creation because we value things that respect our humanity, accepting us as we are (ergonomics of human shape and procedures). T here are no absolutes in good design, just sufficiency. The Singer 401A is as beloved as the 401G even though they follow different shapes and outward appearances, one looking more industrial while the other looks more akin to what a person in 1950 would imagine a spaceship to look like. I am not sure there is an antidote to the difficulty of pricing perfection, but I do know that we need to be cautious of bad design, especially if it is actively keeping us from achieving our goals.

(what pays and how we may find meaning)
What pays (money) is being first to market and solving a novel problem, essentially cornering the market for a short period of time. And this seems to be the primary strategy today: Product market fit -> get paying customers -> improve afterwards
As with all investments, timing is more important than being right. Producing software is a time investment therefore it is implicit that the above laid out strategy is the correct one, even if this seems unsatisfactory to me at first glance but I think there are ways to compensate. I believe it is the duty of any developer to produce the software to sufficiency and design every interaction with intent. It is worthwhile to create the means to ensure the software can live on even after the company ceases to exist to honor the customers trust. In the same way the developers of the Singer 401 entrusted the care and maintenance to the user of the machine by providing a service manual, expecting repairs to be done absent of a "professional". While the sufficiency of that machine was accidental, one could argue, the maintainability and long-lived nature of the product was not. Additinoally, being first to market never kept competition away (Blackberry, MySpace, Netscape, AltaVista).

Software development will stay a worthwhile occupation.



(Written on a 35 year old genuine vintage tool called Vi IMproved, a text editor which can be used to structure thought and text. Why VIM ? I dont even know, the thing was just available from the start, one could say I inherited it. It starts up fast, takes up not any more resources than it needs, does what I tell it to, is easy to use and ergonomic, injects no messages or ads, those kinds of things..)
