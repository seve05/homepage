What is software and the problem with immortality


(a collection of thoughts I used to examine my assumptions about software and its longevity in software while accidentally discovering some principles of design)

(initial observations)
Lately I have been buying a lot of physical tools, mostly vintage and of amazingly high quality in comparison to what is available on the market today. At the same time I'm starting to get a little worried about how software is being perceived and it is leading to some doubtful thoughts and a rather contrasting image compared to the ever-lasting tools with which I can have an immediate, tangible impact. Therefore it is adequate to take some time and think these things through before jumping to any conclusions, as I think part of this confusion is manufactured.
Unlike physical goods modern software can change its form significantly from the state you purchased it in. This is a rather counter-intuitive phenomenon given the static nature of code itself so this needs to be examined. People are changing prices post-contractually, removing features, sometimes removing the purchased product alltogether, adding unskippable ads to your medium and so on. Unlike a saw or a vise software usually cannot be used for a persons lifetime and I'm not convinced it has to be this way.
Sure, physical goods may break down before their useful life is over, however this has not been the standard for all of mankind. A Singer brand sewing machine from 1950 may work just as well now, as it did on the first day it was purchased 76 years ago, if the instructed care was provided to the machine.

(physical vs virtual)
To conclude whether software is comparable to physical products, we need to first establish what a physical product is, what its actual purpose is and then do the same for software. Maybe we can even find examples of software that defy time and trends.
A physical product is a tool to interact with the physical world. Physical products serve humans to fulfill a task: to split wood, traverse space, shelter us from the rain. They allow humans to shape the world to their needs and increase their abilities to accomplish the tasks they want and need to get done.
A virtual product is a product, expressed through information which interacts with the digital world and achieves tasks unfit for the physical world. An unthinkable task for example is to perform very fast search in a large physical database (sure, divide and conquer but there is a limit). Try finding a book in a library as quickly as you can perform a search algorithm on a computer. (Your travel time to and from the library counts as well.) It follows that the purpose of software is to implement digital solutions to physical problems if we are comparing it strictly to tools. These solutions can be general or specific. They might be a calculator implementation, text editor or an e-mail program to interface with a network protocol and lay out data for humans to interact with. These specific programs re-implement basic features and in the same way an email program re-implements a text editor, a roofer's hammer and an engineer's hammer both implement a metalhead with a flat face on a stick. All differentiated by specific features such as weight and shape. Media such as games, working on intellectual problems and also solutions to software issues themselves are being left out in this broad categorization.

(problems with pricing perfection)
One attribute that is unique to software is the ability for it to never break, get dull or develop signs of use. This is also part of what makes software pricing difficult and why some companies appear to make software that is incomplete and broken, in need of repair. Software can be copied and perfectly replicated one to one on another system. You bought one drill bit but you can replicate it as often as you like and do so forever. It never breaks and never needs to be resharpened. That's a good product but a bad one to price to humans. Should that drill bit cost more th an a months rent ? Usually this means people will not want to afford it, even if it is reasonable over a lifetime and technically the correct purchase. Since you can copy it at no cost, one person buys it and supplies everyone with free copies after you crafted, pruned and shaped it meticulously to perfection, costing you many hours of your life. To a certain extent this model has been tried with early physical media by employing copyright protection and distributing activation keys to remedy this effect. The pricing issue is not an issue of the individual but a side effect of a societal model that is incompatible with perfection as is possible in software. We are organized to re-purchase consumables and commodities every month and people usually don't think about the lifetime of an asset or view their purchases as investments at all.

(physical things can be inherited but what about software?)
Software is immortal but the state of our world is not. There are parts of software like open standards, programming languages, typography that can be inherited and live on. Basic programs like Vi, Tar and software in the Linux ecosystem that is correctly written with foresight might still be functional today thanks to C's backward compatability. If a C compiler exists and all the dependencies are included in the program the old software may run, assuming the systems API's are still the same. The software may also not run if a programmer used undefined behavior and trickery to achieve certain functionality like using buffer overflows with 32Bit numbers. The simpler the correct software the more likely it is to still work in my estimation. Old errors may be detected by new compilers such that the compiler won't compile the code even though an older compiler would still have completed the job. Software and hardware that emulate the old system in its entirety may also be available. A special case is the hardware + software combination without third party authentication, which can live on for decades, see the GameBoy ecosystem or older consoles in general. Hand calculators, old CNC machines, firmware and offline, purpose built software falls in that category. Additionally, one could say we inherited the Internet, a rather meaningful piece of technology.

(friend vs foe and good design)
Anyone who helps us in achieving our goals we call friend and anyone who actively defers our ability in doing so we (should) avoid. However we seem to not hold our physical and virtual goods to that same standard even though small inconveniences on a daily basis compound. While any tool is better than no tool, good design serves the human in one task mainly: achieving their goals as effortlessly as possible. This requires pruning and shaping the product until no further cutting out or adding to is necessary or reasonable. The point of sufficiency. That Singer 401G has reached sufficiency for the target audience in a world that didn't change in 75 years. Sure nylon and synthetics were added but sufficiency then meant sewing thick leather and heavy fabric and by accident this extends to todays requirements as well. If this is achieved, an emotional bond to the product will develop and we may find beauty in our creation because we value things that are built with foresight and care for our human constraints (ergonomics of human shape and procedures). There are no absolutes in good design, just sufficiency. The Singer 401A is as beloved as the 401G even though they follow different shapes and outward appearances, one looking more industrial while the other looks more akin to what a person in 1950 would imagine a spaceship to look like. I am not sure there is an antidote to the difficulty of pricing perfection, but I do know that we need to be cautious of bad design, especially if it is actively keeping us from achieving our goals. It is in the hands of the consumer to make these choices but the developers produce the array of choices available.

(what pays and how we may find meaning)
What pays (money) and is exciting is being first to market and solving a novel problem, essentially cornering the market for a short period of time. And this seems to be the primary strategy today: Product market fit -> get the paying customers -> improve the product afterwards
As with all investments, timing is more important than being right. Producing software is a time investment therefore it is implicit that the above laid out strategy is the correct one. I believe it is the duty of any developer to produce the software to sufficiency and design every interaction with intent while also keeping the useful life in mind. Different rules apply to prototypes and educational programming of course, even though I would assert that thinking about sufficiency solves for this as well. Additionally it is worthwhile to create the means to ensure the software can live on even after the company ceases to exist to honor the customers trust. In the same way the developers of the Singer 401 entrusted the care and maintenance to the user of the machine by providing a service manual, expecting repairs to be done absent of a "professional". While the sufficiency of that machine was accidental one could argue, the maintainability and long-lived nature of the product was not.

(on the LLM thing) I currently view language models in the same way I look at the Internet and Search: an extension of human capability. Improved velocitiy of finding information with good accuracy for low entropy queries. I love it because I can learn about anything rapidly, much faster than before and it feels like the flow of information can finally keep up with the amount of questions I have on a daily basis about a range of topics too broad to narrow down in some book or web forum. However the tools are the same for everyone, so what is the differentiator ? In my opinion it is to not miss the last step of the above laid out strategy which is improving the product after already establishing a moat through the first mover advantage. This requires knowing what your product is, how to improve it and therefore probably how it works. Being first to market never kept competition away for as long as there is an advantage to gain (Blackberry, MySpace, Netscape, AltaVista). Maybe I'm out of the loop but I suspect we're witnessing an S curve and heading to the top. Then there is the data bottleneck that needs to be filled somehow, historically by human ingenuity. If we delegate our thinking towards these LLM's entirely who will come up with the next set of training data and will Moore's law keep scaling ? Lastly, touching on some economic principles, if we assume that human desire for improvement and wants in general will stay inelastic, it is also a fair assumption that software won't be a "solved problem" anytime soon. After all, how come the last model is out of date the very second a new one gets released ? If anything this whole process was just a clear indication that copyright and intellectual propery protection does not apply to companies with pockets deep enough while selling a utopian futures/ harbingering impeding purgatory. Truly impressive playbook.

Software development will stay a worthwhile time spend.
